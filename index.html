Ratings in Anime

The rating system in anime, categorizes content based on its suitability for different audiences, considers factors like violence, language, and mature themes and determines who can watch what

In the scatter plot below, anime titles are plotted with their average user scores of the series on the x-axis and the number of members (fans) on the y-axis and as the size, which is scaled logarithmically. Each point's color represents its rating category (such as G, PG, PG-13, R, Rx, Unknown).

The graph displays that anime across all rating categories can achieve high average scores and amass large fanbases. This suggests that while ratings guide viewers on content appropriateness, they do not necessarily predict an anime's popularity or quality.

The diverse distribution highlights that audiences appreciate a wide range of content, and factors like storytelling, character development, and animation quality significantly influence an anime's reception regardless of its rating. Also Fan bases tend to vary more as G to PG series have far more reach.

Completion and Dropping Series

Users completion and dropping off of anime series and specifically how users interact with shows are important to Understanding what is important for consumers and why certain consumers consume certain genres.

However it is important to metion that certain series may have skewed results. This is due to the fact certain series and genres have less episodes when compared to other genres. Also certain genres are just more common than others so they may have a more even distribution of shows in quality when compared to other genres and then due to how My Anime List (MAL) works certain genres are going to be logged on by viewers compared to others.

So for the barbell graph on the x-axis, various anime genres are listed, while the y-axis represents the number of users. For each genre, two key data points are plotted: the number of users who have completed shows (blue points) and those who have dropped shows (red points). A gray line connects these points for each genre, visually illustrating the disparity between completion and drop rates. Additionally, horizontal dashed lines indicate the average number of completions (blue) and drops (red) across all genres, serving as benchmarks for comparison.

Analyzing the visualization, we observe that genres such as Thriller, and Psychological exhibit significantly higher completion rates compared to drop rates, with blue points well above the average completion line and red points somewhat higher than the average drop line. This suggests that series within these genres are particularly engaging, sustaining viewer interest through to the end. The substantial gap between completed and dropped counts indicates strong viewer retention, overall views and satisfaction. In contrast, genres like Kids and Music show a narrower gap between completion and drop rates. This could imply that anime in these genres may not consistently meet viewer expectations or cater to a more niche audience, resulting in higher drop rates. Understanding these patterns is crucial for understanding what generally are good series genres to view.
